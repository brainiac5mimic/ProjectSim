Information on Buoyancy

              The basic explanation of how ships float is their density in comparison to the density of water. If a boat is less dense than water then it will float but if it is more dense then it will sink. Greek mathematician Archimedes was the first to come up for a set of rules concerning buoyancy. He found that the buoyant force is equal to the weight of the water that it displaces. Even though things like aircraft carriers are made of mostly metal, they float because of their large distribution of area. Water is fairly dense so it can exert a strong unbalanced force upward. The upward force of the water on the boat is called upthrust and it is equal to the downward force of the weight of the boat. If a boat weighs less than the total volume of water that it can displace, then it will float. Likewise if it weighs more, than it will sink. The upthrust will be equal and opposite to the weight of the boat until the boat is fully submerged, it cannot displace any more water and therefore the upthrust is constant even when weight is added. When the weight becomes greater than the upthrust, the boat will sink.
