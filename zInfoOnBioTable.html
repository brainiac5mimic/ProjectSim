Ecosystem Background Info

                    An ecosystem is the community of organisms living in an area and the physical factors with which they interact. Energy is transferred between organisms through the trophic levels, levels of organization that categorize species by their main source of nutrition and energy. Because energy is lost through conversion as it moves up the food chain, more producers are needed to sustain a smaller number of consumers and likewise more primary consumers are needed to sustain a smaller number of secondary consumers. This is why biomass pyramids show a sharp decrease in biomass at higher trophic levels because of the ineffectiveness of energy transfer among organisms. The energy expenditures of species higher up on the ecological ladder depend on the production of species below them. Species sizes will fluctuate over time based on the amount of product that is created at the level below them. Food chains are usually complicated and interconnected, forming a more complex food web. A given species could be woven into the web in such a way that it affects many other species in the ecosystem. Natural disasters and other abiotic factors can also affect species growth. The biome that a species are native to will usually closely match their adaptations because of evolution over time. Therefore if the regularity of the biome is disturbed in a short period of time, the species could be greatly affected. Some abiotic factors that can limit the distribution of species is the temperature, water, oxygen, salinity, sunlight, and soil. Species can also be affected by the population density and dispersal rate.
